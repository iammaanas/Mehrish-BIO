What is biomimicry?

Learning from 3.8 billion years of R&D

Biomimicry is the practice of studying and emulating nature's strategies, forms, and processes to solve human design challenges. Nature has been running experiments for billions of years — every surviving organism represents a solution that works.

The word comes from the Greek bios (life) and mimesis (imitation). Coined in its modern scientific sense by Janine Benyus in her 1997 book, biomimicry has since become a formal engineering discipline used by aerospace companies, architects, materials scientists, and product designers.

Structural biomimicry
Copying the physical geometry of natural forms — like the hexagonal honeycomb — to achieve superior mechanical properties in man-made structures.
Process biomimicry
Emulating the processes nature uses — like self-assembly, self-healing, or adaptive growth — to create materials and systems that behave more like living organisms.
Ecosystem biomimicry
Designing systems that mimic the circular, waste-free logic of ecosystems — where the output of one process becomes the input of another. The foundation of circular economy thinking.
The hexagon theorem

Why hexagons? The mathematics of optimality

The honeycomb conjecture — that hexagons are the most efficient way to tile a plane — was first proposed by the Roman scholar Marcus Terentius Varro around 36 BCE. It remained unproven for over 2,000 years until mathematician Thomas Hales formally proved it in 1999.

"The bees have somehow found out that the hexagon is greater than the square and the triangle, and will hold more honey for the same expenditure of material."
— Pappus of Alexandria, 4th century AD, Collection Book V

Three shapes — squares, triangles, and hexagons — can tile a plane with no gaps. Of these, hexagons have the lowest perimeter-to-area ratio (3.72 vs 4.00 for squares vs 4.56 for triangles). Bees use 40% less wax to build the same storage volume with hexagonal cells.

Isoperimetric inequality (2D)
4π A ≤ L²
For any closed curve of length L enclosing area A, equality holds only for a circle. This is why circles have the lowest perimeter-to-area ratio — but circles cannot tile the plane without gaps.
Honeycomb conjecture (Hales, 1999)
P(hex) = 2^(½) · 3^(¼) · A^(½)
Among all tilings of the plane by regions of equal area A, the regular hexagonal tiling has the minimum total perimeter per unit cell.
Relative density of honeycomb core
ρ*/ρₛ = (2/√3)(t/l)
Where ρ* is the honeycomb density, ρₛ is solid wall material density, t is wall thickness, and l is cell edge length. This expresses how much lighter a honeycomb core is than a solid block of the same material.
In-plane stiffness (Gibson & Ashby)
E₁/Eₛ = (t/l)³ · cos θ / (sin²θ · (1+sin θ))
For a regular hexagon (θ = 30°), E₁ = E₂ (isotropic in-plane). Doubling wall thickness increases stiffness eightfold.

About this project

A visual model for understanding structural biomimicry

This interactive tool was built to demonstrate how nature's hexagonal geometry outperforms other tessellation patterns across structural engineering metrics. The simulation uses simplified continuum mechanics models derived from Gibson & Ashby's seminal work on cellular materials.

How the simulation works

The load response models are based on the relative density theory of cellular solids. For each shape, structural factors are derived from the cell geometry: interior angle, wall count, and packing efficiency.

The drop test uses simplified impact mechanics — kinetic energy at impact is computed from weight × g × height. Each shape's absorption capacity scales with material properties, wall thickness, and cell geometry impact factor.

The angle control shifts the impact centroid laterally, amplifying asymmetric stress on cells less aligned with the force vector — revealing how each geometry handles off-axis loading differently.
